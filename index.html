About Me

My name is Lukas Stone, and I am a third-year graduate student in the High Energy Astrophysics Detector and Instrumentation Lab at the Pennsylvania State university. My area of research is in the development and characterization of next-generation X-ray silicon detectors. Specifically, Silicon Hybrid Complementary Metal-Oxide Semiconductor (CMOS) Detectors, or HCDs. HCDs are active pixel sensors (APS) composed of two separate silicon substrates that have been bump-bonded together (hybridized). The top substrate is the silicon absorbing layer optimized for high QE across the soft X-ray bandpass using thick, high-resistivity, fully-depleted silicon. The bottom substrate is the readout integrated circuit (ROIC) optimized for flexible and efficient readout schemes. HCDs pose many advantages over the CCDs used in the majority of current X-ray missions. The ROIC is inherently low-power thanks to the APS design, and more resistant to radiation/micrometeoroid damage. Utilizing in-pixel circuitry, row–column addressing, and the ability to read out pixels independently of one another through parallel output lines, HCDs have significantly faster readout rates than current CCDs, and achieve this with less power and improved radiation hardness. These features make HCDs the next-generation technology required for future small and large-scale X-ray missions.

High Energy Astrophysics Detector and Intrumentation Lab (HEADI)

The HEADI lab has a large array of detector/science programs. Listed are a few:

  • X-ray Detector Development: Designing, testing, calibrating, and characterizing next-generation X-ray detectors that can be used on future small and large-scale X-ray observatories.
  • Space Mission Electronics Development: Design, test, and develop custom low-noise, space-grade electronics.
  • High Energy and Multi-wavelength Analysis: Using Swift, Fermi, VERITAS, and many other observatories we study and model emission from active galactic nuclei, gamma-ray bursts, X-ray binaries, Tidal disruption events, and a number of other exciting and interesting high energy transients.

The Black Hole Coded Aperture Telescope (BlackCAT) CubeSAT

The Black Hole Coded Aperture Telescope (BlackCAT) CubeSat is a large FOV (0.90 steradians) x-ray transient detector and x-ray sky monitor. BlackCAT will operate in the 0.5 – 20 keV bandpass and is optimized for detecting bright, high-z (z >5) gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) from the early universe to study early star formation. Secondary goals of BlackCAT include detecting short GRBs, x-ray counterparts to gravitational wave events, and other x-ray variability sources, e.g., active galactic nuclei, x-ray binaries, and tidal disruption events.
