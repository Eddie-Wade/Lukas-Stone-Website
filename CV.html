About Me

My name is Lukas Stone, and I am a third-year graduate student in the High Energy Astrophysics Detector and Instrumentation Lab at the Pennsylvania State university. My area of research is in the development and characterization of next-generation X-ray silicon detectors. Specifically, Silicon Hybrid Complementary Metal-Oxide Semiconductor (CMOS) Detectors, or HCDs. HCDs are active pixel sensors (APS) composed of two separate silicon substrates that have been bump-bonded together (hybridized). The top substrate is the silicon absorbing layer optimized for high QE across the soft X-ray bandpass using thick, high-resistivity, fully-depleted silicon. The bottom substrate is the readout integrated circuit (ROIC) optimized for flexible and efficient readout schemes. HCDs pose many advantages over the CCDs used in the majority of current X-ray missions. The ROIC is inherently low-power thanks to the APS design, and more resistant to radiation/micrometeoroid damage. Utilizing in-pixel circuitry, rowâ€“column addressing, and the ability to read out pixels independently of one another through parallel output lines, HCDs have significantly faster readout rates than current CCDs, and achieve this with less power and improved radiation hardness. These features make HCDs the next-generation technology required for future small and large-scale X-ray missions.
